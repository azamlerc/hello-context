# Website Intro

Hi, I'm Andrew and this is my website. Apparently I like making lists, so this site is full of them: lists of places, people and things that I find interesting.

When I was 12 my dad challenged me to "broaden my horizons", something that he hadn't done as much as he might have wished. I translated this into my own personal mission statement: "always try to see the world from a different perspective."

## How I Travel

I'm not the kind of person who travels to "do" a city in the tourist sense — extracting value so I can post a few Instagram selfies and move on. I thrive on collecting experiences, deepening my knowledge, and furthering my understanding.

Most of my lists are divided into places I've been and places I want to visit, with a line separating the two. What counts as a visit varies by list. Visiting a country means I've set foot on the ground. Visiting a tower means I've seen it, though some buildings have a badge for making it to the top. On the IKEA list I distinguish between stores where I've gone shopping versus ones I've driven past on the highway.

Some lists are complete in a closed-set sense (EU countries), others are up-to-date snapshots (Apple Stores), and others grow as I go (Airbnbs). I've been to every place on some lists — I'm close to visiting every country in Europe, and I've ticked off all the provinces in Belgium. But I'm not compelled to visit everything. The point is to have fun and learn something along the way. The journey is the destination. If the path is full of thorn bushes, find something else.

## Trains

I'm super into trains, metros, and trams — I think I fell into the pot like Obélix with the magic potion, starting with a visit to the National Capital Trolley Museum as a kid. I have 133+ train-related lists.

One of my favorite things is going to a new city and riding every station on the metro or every stop on the tram network. It's my own way of "doing" a city. It started as a teenager when I took the Vienna U-Bahn just for the fun of it.

Most people experience a metro line the way a geometer thinks about a *line*: if you've never been to either end, it's essentially infinite. If you've been to one end, it's a *ray*. If you've been to both ends, it's a *line segment* — and from that point forward, you always know where you are relative to the ends. Taking the metro for hours just to explore is something I find endlessly enjoyable. Not to mention the victory selfie at the last station.

I'm also drawn to stations with interesting properties: curved platforms (necessitating *Mind the Gap* warnings), cross-platform interchanges, or the Spanish solution with doors opening on both sides. And special track configurations like grand union junctions, where a tram can approach from any of four directions and continue in any other — or tram roundabouts, which serve a similar purpose.

## Geography

I love borders — they have so many interesting artifacts. (When I say "entity" here I mean a country or state.)

**Tripoints** are where three entities meet. Always fun to visit and almost always marked with a monument. **Extreme points** are the northernmost, southernmost, easternmost, and westernmost points of an entity's primary territory — sometimes marked, sometimes not. **Highest points** are the summit of each country or state; requires actual alpinism in Nepal, considerably less so in Luxembourg. **Lowest points** are usually sea level for coastal entities, or a river outflow for landlocked ones — with exceptions like the Dead Sea basin.

**Confluence points** are intersections of integer-degree latitude and longitude lines. There's a community at confluence.org dedicated to visiting them. I've been to around 100. It usually involves walking to the middle of a field or some woods, zeroing the GPS, and shouting yay. Sometimes there are footprints or trampled grass, occasionally a sign, very rarely a monument.

## Bingo

I have bingo cards for systematically exploring regions. Each card covers a set of objectives: capital cities, extreme points, highest and lowest points, tripoints, confluence points, and UNESCO World Heritage Sites. I've made solid progress in the northeastern US and western Europe. I did the Baltics in a week. Next up: the Balkans.

## UNESCO World Heritage Sites

The official UN list of cultural and natural sites with enduring intrinsic value. Yes, the Taj Mahal is on there, but there's a lot of genuinely good stuff too. Of the 1,200+ sites, I've curated 400+ in my list and visited around half of those.

## Bucket List

The bucket list concept is a bit cliché — everyone's got the same overcrowded tourist greatest hits. I don't have one list; I have 300+ lists with wiki links, locations, and stats. That said, I made an über bucket list mostly to make fun of the concept. It's tongue-in-cheek. Sometimes I add things after visiting because they turned out to be genuinely great — like BRT in Bogotá or the Holmenkollen Line in Oslo.

## The Database

Most entries link to Wikipedia. I read Wikipedia voraciously — there are upwards of 20,000 linked entries. My database stores the intro paragraph of each Wikipedia page and supports semantic search and finding similar entries. Most places also have coordinates, and many pages have maps showing green pins for places I've been and red pins for places I want to visit. The database supports radius searches to find things nearby.
